Explore the Texas Experience

The cultural mosaic of Texas is a dynamic blend of influences, from the deep roots of its indigenous peoples to the vibrant contributions of Spanish, Mexican, and European settlers. This amalgamation has given rise to a state where traditions are celebrated with pride, where rodeos and barbecue festivals echo the spirit of the Wild West, and where modern urban centers pulse with the energy of innovation and multiculturalism.

The history of Texas is a narrative etched in time, marked by tales of Spanish explorers, missions, and the struggle for independence. The echoes of the Battle of the Alamo and the iconic figures like Sam Houston resonate through the annals of Texan history, creating a sense of pride and identity among its residents. From the days of the Republic of Texas to its eventual annexation into the United States, each chapter contributes to the unique character of the Lone Star State.

In every corner of Texas, from the bustling metropolises of Houston and Dallas to the serene countryside, there's an undeniable sense of pride in being a Texan. It's a state where hospitality is as vast as its horizons, where traditions are passed down through generations, and where the spirit of independence and resilience continues to shape its ever-evolving narrative.

Six Flags over Texas

The Six Flags over Texas are the six flags that have flown over the U.S. state of Texas since 1519, when Alonso Álvarez de Pineda claimed Texas in the name of the Spanish Empire. The flags are the emblems of Spain (1519–1685; 1690–1821), France (1685–1690), Mexico (1821–1836), the Republic of Texas (1836–1845), the Confederate States of America (1861–1865), and the United States of America (1845–1861; 1865–present day).

Spain (1519–1685; 1690–1821)

The first flag belonged to Spain, which ruled most of Texas from 1519 to 1685 and 1690 to 1821. Spain was the first European country to claim and control the area of Texas. The first mission established in Texas was Corpus Christi de la Ysleta, founded in 1682 by Franciscan friars serving at the Jesuit missions in northern Mexico. In 1684, Spanish explorer Juan Domínguez de Mendoza established the first Spanish fortification in Texas, Presidio San Juan Bautista, on the Rio Grande near modern Eagle Pass. The new fort was named in honor of the mission that Mendoza had established nearby, San Juan Bautista. In 1689, Spanish explorer Alonso de León, who had been searching for the French settlement La Salle founded in 1685, crossed into Texas from Mexico and rediscovered the ruins of the French fort. He buried a lead plate inscribed with the royal arms of France and Spain at the site, claiming the territory for his king and country. The Spanish village of Nacogdoches was founded on the site of the French settlement in 1779. In 1803, the Louisiana Purchase by Thomas Jefferson brought the United States into control of the Texas claim by right of its purchase of the French claim on the continent.

France (1685–1690)

The second flag belonged to France, which ruled most of Texas from 1685 to 1690. France was in control of the territory from 1685 to 1690, after the French admiral Jean-Baptiste Le Moyne, Sieur de Bienville claimed the territory as French in 1685. The French founded the short lived Fort Saint Louis in 1685 as part of La Salle's colony. The colony was killed off by Native Americans after three years of occupation. In 1690, Spanish authorities, led by Alonso de León, found the French colony in ruins and buried the remains of the fort's dead defenders, planting a cross over the grave and claiming the territory for Spain.

Mexico (1821–1836)

The third flag belonged to Mexico, which ruled Texas from 1821 to 1836. Mexico was in control of the territory from 1821 until 1836 after the Mexican War of Independence. Mexico had become an independent nation in 1821, after gaining its independence from Spain. Mexico was in control of the territory of Texas until 1836 when Texas gained independence from Mexico.

Republic of Texas (1836–1845)

The fourth flag belonged to the Republic of Texas from 1836 to 1845. Texas declared independence from Mexico in 1836. The Republic of Texas was formed and its flag was first raised on March 2, 1836. The Republic of Texas was annexed to the United States on December 29, 1845, after Texas voters approved annexation in a statewide referendum.

Confederate States of America (1861–1865)

The fifth flag belonged to the Confederate States of America from 1861 to 1865. Texas seceded from the United States in 1861 and joined the Confederate States of America. The Confederate States of America was defeated in 1865 and the United States was restored.

United States of America (1845–1861; 1865–present day)

The sixth and current flag was adopted by the state legislature on August 31, 1933, and approved by Texas voters in a referendum on September 5, 1933. The six flags are also shown on the reverse of the Seal of Texas.

Fun Facts

  • Flag Variety: Texas is the only state to have the flags of 6 different nations fly over it.
  • Treaty Entry: Texas is the only state to enter the United States by treaty instead of territorial annexation.
  • Space Voting: Texas is the only state that permits residents to cast absentee ballots from space.
  • Etymology: Did you know that the word "Texas" comes from the Caddo language, meaning "friends" or "allies"?
  • Lone Star State: Texas is often referred to as the "Lone Star State" due to the single star on its flag, symbolizing its independent spirit.
  • Size Matters: Texas is the second-largest state in the United States, both in terms of land area and population. It's so vast that it could fit several smaller states within its borders.
  • Oil Rich: Texas is a major player in the oil industry. It produces more crude oil than any other state in the U.S., making it a significant contributor to the nation's energy production.
  • Cattle Country: Known for its ranches and cowboy culture, Texas has the largest cattle population in the United States. You'll find numerous ranches and rodeos showcasing this proud tradition.
  • NASA Headquarters: Houston, Texas, is home to the Lyndon B. Johnson Space Center, the headquarters for NASA's human spaceflight program. It played a crucial role in the Apollo moon missions.
  • Biggest City: Houston is not only known for its connection to space exploration but is also the most populous city in Texas. It's a melting pot of diverse cultures and cuisines.
  • State Flower: The bluebonnet is the state flower of Texas. These beautiful wildflowers bloom in spring, covering fields and highways with vibrant blue hues.
  • Music Capital: Austin, the state capital, is often referred to as the "Live Music Capital of the World." It hosts numerous music festivals and has a vibrant live music scene.
  • Mexican Influence: Texas has a rich Mexican cultural influence, particularly evident in its cuisine. Tex-Mex dishes, combining Texan and Mexican flavors, are popular throughout the state.
  • Football Frenzy: Football is a big deal in Texas. High school football games draw massive crowds, and the state is home to legendary football teams and rivalries.
